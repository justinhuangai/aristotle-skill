**Nicomachean Ethics**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

By Aristotle [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Written 350 B.C.E

Translated by W. D. Ross

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)1 [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Every art and every inquiry, and similarly every action and pursuit, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)is thought to aim at some good; and for this reason the good has rightly [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)been declared to be that at which all things aim. But a certain difference [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)is found among ends; some are activities, others are products apart from [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the activities that produce them. Where there are ends apart from the actions, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)it is the nature of the products to be better than the activities. Now, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)as there are many actions, arts, and sciences, their ends also are many; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the end of the medical art is health, that of shipbuilding a vessel, that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of strategy victory, that of economics wealth. But where such arts fall [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)under a single capacity- as bridle-making and the other arts concerned [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)with the equipment of horses fall under the art of riding, and this and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)every military action under strategy, in the same way other arts fall under [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)yet others- in all of these the ends of the master arts are to be preferred [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)to all the subordinate ends; for it is for the sake of the former that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the latter are pursued. It makes no difference whether the activities themselves [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)are the ends of the actions, or something else apart from the activities, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)as in the case of the sciences just mentioned. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

2 [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

If, then, there is some end of the things we do, which we desire [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)for its own sake (everything else being desired for the sake of this), [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and if we do not choose everything for the sake of something else (for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)at that rate the process would go on to infinity, so that our desire would [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)be empty and vain), clearly this must be the good and the chief good. Will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)not the knowledge of it, then, have a great influence on life? Shall we [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)not, like archers who have a mark to aim at, be more likely to hit upon [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)what is right? If so, we must try, in outline at least, to determine what [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)it is, and of which of the sciences or capacities it is the object. It [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)would seem to belong to the most authoritative art and that which is most [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)truly the master art. And politics appears to be of this nature; for it [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)is this that ordains which of the sciences should be studied in a state, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and which each class of citizens should learn and up to what point they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)should learn them; and we see even the most highly esteemed of capacities [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)to fall under this, e.g. strategy, economics, rhetoric; now, since politics [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)uses the rest of the sciences, and since, again, it legislates as to what [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)we are to do and what we are to abstain from, the end of this science must [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)include those of the others, so that this end must be the good for man. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)For even if the end is the same for a single man and for a state, that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of the state seems at all events something greater and more complete whether [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)to attain or to preserve; though it is worth while to attain the end merely [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)for one man, it is finer and more godlike to attain it for a nation or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)for city-states. These, then, are the ends at which our inquiry aims, since [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)it is political science, in one sense of that term. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

3 [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Our discussion will be adequate if it has as much clearness as [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the subject-matter admits of, for precision is not to be sought for alike [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)in all discussions, any more than in all the products of the crafts. Now [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)fine and just actions, which political science investigates, admit of much [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)variety and fluctuation of opinion, so that they may be thought to exist [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)only by convention, and not by nature. And goods also give rise to a similar [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)fluctuation because they bring harm to many people; for before now men [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)have been undone by reason of their wealth, and others by reason of their [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)courage. We must be content, then, in speaking of such subjects and with [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)such premisses to indicate the truth roughly and in outline, and in speaking [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)about things which are only for the most part true and with premisses of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the same kind to reach conclusions that are no better. In the same spirit, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)therefore, should each type of statement be received; for it is the mark [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of an educated man to look for precision in each class of things just so [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)far as the nature of the subject admits; it is evidently equally foolish [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)to accept probable reasoning from a mathematician and to demand from a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)rhetorician scientific proofs. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Now each man judges well the things he knows, and of these he is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)a good judge. And so the man who has been educated in a subject is a good [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)judge of that subject, and the man who has received an all-round education [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)is a good judge in general. Hence a young man is not a proper hearer of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)lectures on political science; for he is inexperienced in the actions that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)occur in life, but its discussions start from these and are about these; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and, further, since he tends to follow his passions, his study will be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)vain and unprofitable, because the end aimed at is not knowledge but action. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)And it makes no difference whether he is young in years or youthful in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)character; the defect does not depend on time, but on his living, and pursuing [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)each successive object, as passion directs. For to such persons, as to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the incontinent, knowledge brings no profit; but to those who desire and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)act in accordance with a rational principle knowledge about such matters [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)will be of great benefit. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

These remarks about the student, the sort of treatment to be expected, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and the purpose of the inquiry, may be taken as our [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)preface. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

4 [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Let us resume our inquiry and state, in view of the fact that all [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)knowledge and every pursuit aims at some good, what it is that we say political [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)science aims at and what is the highest of all goods achievable by action. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)Verbally there is very general agreement; for both the general run of men [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and people of superior refinement say that it is happiness, and identify [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)living well and doing well with being happy; but with regard to what happiness [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)is they differ, and the many do not give the same account as the wise. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)For the former think it is some plain and obvious thing, like pleasure, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)wealth, or honour; they differ, however, from one another- and often even [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the same man identifies it with different things, with health when he is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)ill, with wealth when he is poor; but, conscious of their ignorance, they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)admire those who proclaim some great ideal that is above their comprehension. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)Now some thought that apart from these many goods there is another which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)is self-subsistent and causes the goodness of all these as well. To examine [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)all the opinions that have been held were perhaps somewhat fruitless; enough [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)to examine those that are most prevalent or that seem to be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)arguable. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Let us not fail to notice, however, that there is a difference [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)between arguments from and those to the first principles. For Plato, too, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)was right in raising this question and asking, as he used to do, 'are we [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)on the way from or to the first principles?' There is a difference, as [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)there is in a race-course between the course from the judges to the turning-point [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and the way back. For, while we must begin with what is known, things are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)objects of knowledge in two senses- some to us, some without qualification. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)Presumably, then, we must begin with things known to us. Hence any one [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)who is to listen intelligently to lectures about what is noble and just, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and generally, about the subjects of political science must have been brought [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)up in good habits. For the fact is the starting-point, and if this is sufficiently [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)plain to him, he will not at the start need the reason as well; and the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)man who has been well brought up has or can easily get startingpoints. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)And as for him who neither has nor can get them, let him hear the words [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of Hesiod: [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Far best is he who knows all things himself; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Good, he that hearkens when men counsel right; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

But he who neither knows, nor lays to heart [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Another's wisdom, is a useless wight. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

5 [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Let us, however, resume our discussion from the point at which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)we digressed. To judge from the lives that men lead, most men, and men [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of the most vulgar type, seem (not without some ground) to identify the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)good, or happiness, with pleasure; which is the reason why they love the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)life of enjoyment. For there are, we may say, three prominent types of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)life- that just mentioned, the political, and thirdly the contemplative [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)life. Now the mass of mankind are evidently quite slavish in their tastes, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)preferring a life suitable to beasts, but they get some ground for their [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)view from the fact that many of those in high places share the tastes of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)Sardanapallus. A consideration of the prominent types of life shows that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)people of superior refinement and of active disposition identify happiness [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)with honour; for this is, roughly speaking, the end of the political life. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)But it seems too superficial to be what we are looking for, since it is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)thought to depend on those who bestow honour rather than on him who receives [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)it, but the good we divine to be something proper to a man and not easily [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)taken from him. Further, men seem to pursue honour in order that they may [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)be assured of their goodness; at least it is by men of practical wisdom [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)that they seek to be honoured, and among those who know them, and on the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)ground of their virtue; clearly, then, according to them, at any rate, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)virtue is better. And perhaps one might even suppose this to be, rather [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)than honour, the end of the political life. But even this appears somewhat [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)incomplete; for possession of virtue seems actually compatible with being [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)asleep, or with lifelong inactivity, and, further, with the greatest sufferings [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and misfortunes; but a man who was living so no one would call happy, unless [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)he were maintaining a thesis at all costs. But enough of this; for the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)subject has been sufficiently treated even in the current discussions. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)Third comes the contemplative life, which we shall consider [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)later. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

The life of money-making is one undertaken under compulsion, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)wealth is evidently not the good we are seeking; for it is merely useful [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and for the sake of something else. And so one might rather take the aforenamed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)objects to be ends; for they are loved for themselves. But it is evident [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)that not even these are ends; yet many arguments have been thrown away [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)in support of them. Let us leave this subject, then. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

6 [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

We had perhaps better consider the universal good and discuss thoroughly [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)what is meant by it, although such an inquiry is made an uphill one by [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the fact that the Forms have been introduced by friends of our own. Yet [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)it would perhaps be thought to be better, indeed to be our duty, for the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)sake of maintaining the truth even to destroy what touches us closely, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)especially as we are philosophers or lovers of wisdom; for, while both [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)are dear, piety requires us to honour truth above our [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)friends. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

The men who introduced this doctrine did not posit Ideas of classes [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)within which they recognized priority and posteriority (which is the reason [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)why they did not maintain the existence of an Idea embracing all numbers); [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)but the term 'good' is used both in the category of substance and in that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of quality and in that of relation, and that which is per se, i.e. substance, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)is prior in nature to the relative (for the latter is like an off shoot [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and accident of being); so that there could not be a common Idea set over [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)all these goods. Further, since 'good' has as many senses as 'being' (for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)it is predicated both in the category of substance, as of God and of reason, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and in quality, i.e. of the virtues, and in quantity, i.e. of that which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)is moderate, and in relation, i.e. of the useful, and in time, i.e. of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the right opportunity, and in place, i.e. of the right locality and the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)like), clearly it cannot be something universally present in all cases [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and single; for then it could not have been predicated in all the categories [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)but in one only. Further, since of the things answering to one Idea there [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)is one science, there would have been one science of all the goods; but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)as it is there are many sciences even of the things that fall under one [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)category, e.g. of opportunity, for opportunity in war is studied by strategics [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and in disease by medicine, and the moderate in food is studied by medicine [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and in exercise by the science of gymnastics. And one might ask the question, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)what in the world they mean by 'a thing itself', is (as is the case) in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)'man himself' and in a particular man the account of man is one and the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)same. For in so far as they are man, they will in no respect differ; and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)if this is so, neither will 'good itself' and particular goods, in so far [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)as they are good. But again it will not be good any the more for being [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)eternal, since that which lasts long is no whiter than that which perishes [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)in a day. The Pythagoreans seem to give a more plausible account of the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)good, when they place the one in the column of goods; and it is they that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)Speusippus seems to have followed. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

But let us discuss these matters elsewhere; an objection to what [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)we have said, however, may be discerned in the fact that the Platonists [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)have not been speaking about all goods, and that the goods that are pursued [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and loved for themselves are called good by reference to a single Form, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)while those which tend to produce or to preserve these somehow or to prevent [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)their contraries are called so by reference to these, and in a secondary [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)sense. Clearly, then, goods must be spoken of in two ways, and some must [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)be good in themselves, the others by reason of these. Let us separate, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)then, things good in themselves from things useful, and consider whether [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the former are called good by reference to a single Idea. What sort of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)goods would one call good in themselves? Is it those that are pursued even [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)when isolated from others, such as intelligence, sight, and certain pleasures [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and honours? Certainly, if we pursue these also for the sake of something [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)else, yet one would place them among things good in themselves. Or is nothing [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)other than the Idea of good good in itself? In that case the Form will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)be empty. But if the things we have named are also things good in themselves, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the account of the good will have to appear as something identical in them [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)all, as that of whiteness is identical in snow and in white lead. But of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)honour, wisdom, and pleasure, just in respect of their goodness, the accounts [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)are distinct and diverse. The good, therefore, is not some common element [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)answering to one Idea. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

But what then do we mean by the good? It is surely not like the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)things that only chance to have the same name. Are goods one, then, by [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)being derived from one good or by all contributing to one good, or are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)they rather one by analogy? Certainly as sight is in the body, so is reason [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)in the soul, and so on in other cases. But perhaps these subjects had better [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)be dismissed for the present; for perfect precision about them would be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)more appropriate to another branch of philosophy. And similarly with regard [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)to the Idea; even if there is some one good which is universally predicable [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of goods or is capable of separate and independent existence, clearly it [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)could not be achieved or attained by man; but we are now seeking something [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)attainable. Perhaps, however, some one might think it worth while to recognize [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)this with a view to the goods that are attainable and achievable; for having [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)this as a sort of pattern we shall know better the goods that are good [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)for us, and if we know them shall attain them. This argument has some plausibility, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)but seems to clash with the procedure of the sciences; for all of these, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)though they aim at some good and seek to supply the deficiency of it, leave [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)on one side the knowledge of the good. Yet that all the exponents of the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)arts should be ignorant of, and should not even seek, so great an aid is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)not probable. It is hard, too, to see how a weaver or a carpenter will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)be benefited in regard to his own craft by knowing this 'good itself', [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)or how the man who has viewed the Idea itself will be a better doctor or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)general thereby. For a doctor seems not even to study health in this way, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)but the health of man, or perhaps rather the health of a particular man; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)it is individuals that he is healing. But enough of these [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)topics. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

7 [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Let us again return to the good we are seeking, and ask what it [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)can be. It seems different in different actions and arts; it is different [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)in medicine, in strategy, and in the other arts likewise. What then is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the good of each? Surely that for whose sake everything else is done. In [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)medicine this is health, in strategy victory, in architecture a house, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)in any other sphere something else, and in every action and pursuit the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)end; for it is for the sake of this that all men do whatever else they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)do. Therefore, if there is an end for all that we do, this will be the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)good achievable by action, and if there are more than one, these will be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the goods achievable by action. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

So the argument has by a different course reached the same point; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)but we must try to state this even more clearly. Since there are evidently [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)more than one end, and we choose some of these (e.g. wealth, flutes, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)in general instruments) for the sake of something else, clearly not all [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)ends are final ends; but the chief good is evidently something final. Therefore, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)if there is only one final end, this will be what we are seeking, and if [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)there are more than one, the most final of these will be what we are seeking. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)Now we call that which is in itself worthy of pursuit more final than that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)which is worthy of pursuit for the sake of something else, and that which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)is never desirable for the sake of something else more final than the things [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)that are desirable both in themselves and for the sake of that other thing, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and therefore we call final without qualification that which is always [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)desirable in itself and never for the sake of something [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)else. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Now such a thing happiness, above all else, is held to be; for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)this we choose always for self and never for the sake of something else, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)but honour, pleasure, reason, and every virtue we choose indeed for themselves [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)(for if nothing resulted from them we should still choose each of them), [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)but we choose them also for the sake of happiness, judging that by means [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of them we shall be happy. Happiness, on the other hand, no one chooses [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)for the sake of these, nor, in general, for anything other than [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)itself. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

From the point of view of self-sufficiency the same result seems [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)to follow; for the final good is thought to be self-sufficient. Now by [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)self-sufficient we do not mean that which is sufficient for a man by himself, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)for one who lives a solitary life, but also for parents, children, wife, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and in general for his friends and fellow citizens, since man is born for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)citizenship. But some limit must be set to this; for if we extend our requirement [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)to ancestors and descendants and friends' friends we are in for an infinite [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)series. Let us examine this question, however, on another occasion; the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)self-sufficient we now define as that which when isolated makes life desirable [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and lacking in nothing; and such we think happiness to be; and further [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)we think it most desirable of all things, without being counted as one [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)good thing among others- if it were so counted it would clearly be made [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)more desirable by the addition of even the least of goods; for that which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)is added becomes an excess of goods, and of goods the greater is always [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)more desirable. Happiness, then, is something final and self-sufficient, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and is the end of action. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Presumably, however, to say that happiness is the chief good seems [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)a platitude, and a clearer account of what it is still desired. This might [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)perhaps be given, if we could first ascertain the function of man. For [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)just as for a flute-player, a sculptor, or an artist, and, in general, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)for all things that have a function or activity, the good and the 'well' [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)is thought to reside in the function, so would it seem to be for man, if [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)he has a function. Have the carpenter, then, and the tanner certain functions [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)or activities, and has man none? Is he born without a function? Or as eye, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)hand, foot, and in general each of the parts evidently has a function, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)may one lay it down that man similarly has a function apart from all these? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)What then can this be? Life seems to be common even to plants, but we are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)seeking what is peculiar to man. Let us exclude, therefore, the life of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)nutrition and growth. Next there would be a life of perception, but it [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)also seems to be common even to the horse, the ox, and every animal. There [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)remains, then, an active life of the element that has a rational principle; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of this, one part has such a principle in the sense of being obedient to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)one, the other in the sense of possessing one and exercising thought. And, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)as 'life of the rational element' also has two meanings, we must state [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)that life in the sense of activity is what we mean; for this seems to be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the more proper sense of the term. Now if the function of man is an activity [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of soul which follows or implies a rational principle, and if we say 'so-and-so-and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)'a good so-and-so' have a function which is the same in kind, e.g. a lyre, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and a good lyre-player, and so without qualification in all cases, eminence [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)in respect of goodness being idded to the name of the function (for the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)function of a lyre-player is to play the lyre, and that of a good lyre-player [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)is to do so well): if this is the case, and we state the function of man [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)to be a certain kind of life, and this to be an activity or actions of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the soul implying a rational principle, and the function of a good man [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)to be the good and noble performance of these, and if any action is well [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)performed when it is performed in accordance with the appropriate excellence: [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)if this is the case, human good turns out to be activity of soul in accordance [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)with virtue, and if there are more than one virtue, in accordance with [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the best and most complete. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

But we must add 'in a complete life.' For one swallow does not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)make a summer, nor does one day; and so too one day, or a short time, does [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)not make a man blessed and happy. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Let this serve as an outline of the good; for we must presumably [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)first sketch it roughly, and then later fill in the details. But it would [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)seem that any one is capable of carrying on and articulating what has once [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)been well outlined, and that time is a good discoverer or partner in such [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)a work; to which facts the advances of the arts are due; for any one can [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)add what is lacking. And we must also remember what has been said before, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and not look for precision in all things alike, but in each class of things [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)such precision as accords with the subject-matter, and so much as is appropriate [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)to the inquiry. For a carpenter and a geometer investigate the right angle [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)in different ways; the former does so in so far as the right angle is useful [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)for his work, while the latter inquires what it is or what sort of thing [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)it is; for he is a spectator of the truth. We must act in the same way, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)then, in all other matters as well, that our main task may not be subordinated [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)to minor questions. Nor must we demand the cause in all matters alike; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)it is enough in some cases that the fact be well established, as in the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)case of the first principles; the fact is the primary thing or first principle. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)Now of first principles we see some by induction, some by perception, some [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)by a certain habituation, and others too in other ways. But each set of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)principles we must try to investigate in the natural way, and we must take [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)pains to state them definitely, since they have a great influence on what [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)follows. For the beginning is thought to be more than half of the whole, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and many of the questions we ask are cleared up by it. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

8 [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

We must consider it, however, in the light not only of our conclusion [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and our premisses, but also of what is commonly said about it; for with [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)a true view all the data harmonize, but with a false one the facts soon [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)clash. Now goods have been divided into three classes, and some are described [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)as external, others as relating to soul or to body; we call those that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)relate to soul most properly and truly goods, and psychical actions and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)activities we class as relating to soul. Therefore our account must be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)sound, at least according to this view, which is an old one and agreed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)on by philosophers. It is correct also in that we identify the end with [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)certain actions and activities; for thus it falls among goods of the soul [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and not among external goods. Another belief which harmonizes with our [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)account is that the happy man lives well and does well; for we have practically [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)defined happiness as a sort of good life and good action. The characteristics [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)that are looked for in happiness seem also, all of them, to belong to what [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)we have defined happiness as being. For some identify happiness with virtue, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)some with practical wisdom, others with a kind of philosophic wisdom, others [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)with these, or one of these, accompanied by pleasure or not without pleasure; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)while others include also external prosperity. Now some of these views [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)have been held by many men and men of old, others by a few eminent persons; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and it is not probable that either of these should be entirely mistaken, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)but rather that they should be right in at least some one respect or even [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)in most respects. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

With those who identify happiness with virtue or some one virtue [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)our account is in harmony; for to virtue belongs virtuous activity. But [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)it makes, perhaps, no small difference whether we place the chief good [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)in possession or in use, in state of mind or in activity. For the state [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of mind may exist without producing any good result, as in a man who is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)asleep or in some other way quite inactive, but the activity cannot; for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)one who has the activity will of necessity be acting, and acting well. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)And as in the Olympic Games it is not the most beautiful and the strongest [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)that are crowned but those who compete (for it is some of these that are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)victorious), so those who act win, and rightly win, the noble and good [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)things in life. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Their life is also in itself pleasant. For pleasure is a state [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of soul, and to each man that which he is said to be a lover of is pleasant; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)e.g. not only is a horse pleasant to the lover of horses, and a spectacle [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)to the lover of sights, but also in the same way just acts are pleasant [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)to the lover of justice and in general virtuous acts to the lover of virtue. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)Now for most men their pleasures are in conflict with one another because [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)these are not by nature pleasant, but the lovers of what is noble find [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)pleasant the things that are by nature pleasant; and virtuous actions are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)such, so that these are pleasant for such men as well as in their own nature. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)Their life, therefore, has no further need of pleasure as a sort of adventitious [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)charm, but has its pleasure in itself. For, besides what we have said, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the man who does not rejoice in noble actions is not even good; since no [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)one would call a man just who did not enjoy acting justly, nor any man [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)liberal who did not enjoy liberal actions; and similarly in all other cases. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)If this is so, virtuous actions must be in themselves pleasant. But they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)are also good and noble, and have each of these attributes in the highest [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)degree, since the good man judges well about these attributes; his judgement [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)is such as we have described. Happiness then is the best, noblest, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)most pleasant thing in the world, and these attributes are not severed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)as in the inscription at Delos- [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Most noble is that which is justest, and best is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)health; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

But pleasantest is it to win what we love. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

For all these properties belong to the best activities; and these, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)or one- the best- of these, we identify with happiness. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Yet evidently, as we said, it needs the external goods as well; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)for it is impossible, or not easy, to do noble acts without the proper [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)equipment. In many actions we use friends and riches and political power [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)as instruments; and there are some things the lack of which takes the lustre [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)from happiness, as good birth, goodly children, beauty; for the man who [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)is very ugly in appearance or ill-born or solitary and childless is not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)very likely to be happy, and perhaps a man would be still less likely if [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)he had thoroughly bad children or friends or had lost good children or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)friends by death. As we said, then, happiness seems to need this sort of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)prosperity in addition; for which reason some identify happiness with good [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)fortune, though others identify it with virtue. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

9 [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

For this reason also the question is asked, whether happiness is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)to be acquired by learning or by habituation or some other sort of training, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)or comes in virtue of some divine providence or again by chance. Now if [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)there is any gift of the gods to men, it is reasonable that happiness should [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)be god-given, and most surely god-given of all human things inasmuch as [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)it is the best. But this question would perhaps be more appropriate to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)another inquiry; happiness seems, however, even if it is not god-sent but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)comes as a result of virtue and some process of learning or training, to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)be among the most godlike things; for that which is the prize and end of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)virtue seems to be the best thing in the world, and something godlike and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)blessed. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

It will also on this view be very generally shared; for all who [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)are not maimed as regards their potentiality for virtue may win it by a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)certain kind of study and care. But if it is better to be happy thus than [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)by chance, it is reasonable that the facts should be so, since everything [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)that depends on the action of nature is by nature as good as it can be, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and similarly everything that depends on art or any rational cause, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)especially if it depends on the best of all causes. To entrust to chance [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)what is greatest and most noble would be a very defective [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)arrangement. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

The answer to the question we are asking is plain also from the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)definition of happiness; for it has been said to be a virtuous activity [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of soul, of a certain kind. Of the remaining goods, some must necessarily [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)pre-exist as conditions of happiness, and others are naturally co-operative [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and useful as instruments. And this will be found to agree with what we [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)said at the outset; for we stated the end of political science to be the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)best end, and political science spends most of its pains on making the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)citizens to be of a certain character, viz. good and capable of noble [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)acts. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

It is natural, then, that we call neither ox nor horse nor any [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)other of the animals happy; for none of them is capable of sharing in such [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)activity. For this reason also a boy is not happy; for he is not yet capable [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of such acts, owing to his age; and boys who are called happy are being [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)congratulated by reason of the hopes we have for them. For there is required, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)as we said, not only complete virtue but also a complete life, since many [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)changes occur in life, and all manner of chances, and the most prosperous [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)may fall into great misfortunes in old age, as is told of Priam in the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)Trojan Cycle; and one who has experienced such chances and has ended wretchedly [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)no one calls happy. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

10 [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Must no one at all, then, be called happy while he lives; must [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)we, as Solon says, see the end? Even if we are to lay down this doctrine, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)is it also the case that a man is happy when he is dead? Or is not this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)quite absurd, especially for us who say that happiness is an activity? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)But if we do not call the dead man happy, and if Solon does not mean this, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)but that one can then safely call a man blessed as being at last beyond [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)evils and misfortunes, this also affords matter for discussion; for both [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)evil and good are thought to exist for a dead man, as much as for one who [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)is alive but not aware of them; e.g. honours and dishonours and the good [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)or bad fortunes of children and in general of descendants. And this also [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)presents a problem; for though a man has lived happily up to old age and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)has had a death worthy of his life, many reverses may befall his descendants- [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)some of them may be good and attain the life they deserve, while with others [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the opposite may be the case; and clearly too the degrees of relationship [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)between them and their ancestors may vary indefinitely. It would be odd, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)then, if the dead man were to share in these changes and become at one [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)time happy, at another wretched; while it would also be odd if the fortunes [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of the descendants did not for some time have some effect on the happiness [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of their ancestors. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

But we must return to our first difficulty; for perhaps by a consideration [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of it our present problem might be solved. Now if we must see the end and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)only then call a man happy, not as being happy but as having been so before, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)surely this is a paradox, that when he is happy the attribute that belongs [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)to him is not to be truly predicated of him because we do not wish to call [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)living men happy, on account of the changes that may befall them, and because [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)we have assumed happiness to be something permanent and by no means easily [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)changed, while a single man may suffer many turns of fortune's wheel. For [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)clearly if we were to keep pace with his fortunes, we should often call [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the same man happy and again wretched, making the happy man out to be chameleon [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and insecurely based. Or is this keeping pace with his fortunes quite wrong? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)Success or failure in life does not depend on these, but human life, as [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)we said, needs these as mere additions, while virtuous activities or their [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)opposites are what constitute happiness or the reverse. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

The question we have now discussed confirms our definition. For [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)no function of man has so much permanence as virtuous activities (these [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)are thought to be more durable even than knowledge of the sciences), and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of these themselves the most valuable are more durable because those who [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)are happy spend their life most readily and most continuously in these; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)for this seems to be the reason why we do not forget them. The attribute [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)in question, then, will belong to the happy man, and he will be happy throughout [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)his life; for always, or by preference to everything else, he will be engaged [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)in virtuous action and contemplation, and he will bear the chances of life [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)most nobly and altogether decorously, if he is 'truly good' and 'foursquare [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)beyond reproach'. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Now many events happen by chance, and events differing in importance; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)small pieces of good fortune or of its opposite clearly do not weigh down [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the scales of life one way or the other, but a multitude of great events [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)if they turn out well will make life happier (for not only are they themselves [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)such as to add beauty to life, but the way a man deals with them may be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)noble and good), while if they turn out ill they crush and maim happiness; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)for they both bring pain with them and hinder many activities. Yet even [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)in these nobility shines through, when a man bears with resignation many [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)great misfortunes, not through insensibility to pain but through nobility [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and greatness of soul. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

If activities are, as we said, what gives life its character, no [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)happy man can become miserable; for he will never do the acts that are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)hateful and mean. For the man who is truly good and wise, we think, bears [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)all the chances life becomingly and always makes the best of circumstances, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)as a good general makes the best military use of the army at his command [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)and a good shoemaker makes the best shoes out of the hides that are given [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)him; and so with all other craftsmen. And if this is the case, the happy [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)man can never become miserable; though he will not reach blessedness, if [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)he meet with fortunes like those of Priam. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

Nor, again, is he many-coloured and changeable; for neither will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)he be moved from his happy state easily or by any ordinary misadventures, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)but only by many great ones, nor, if he has had many great misadventures, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)will he recover his happiness in a short time, but if at all, only in a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)long and complete one in which he has attained many splendid [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)successes. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

When then should we not say that he is happy who is active in accordance [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)with complete virtue and is sufficiently equipped with external goods, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)not for some chance period but throughout a complete life? Or must we add [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)'and who is destined to live thus and die as befits his life'? Certainly [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the future is obscure to us, while happiness, we claim, is an end and something [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)in every way final. If so, we shall call happy those among living men in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)whom these conditions are, and are to be, fulfilled- but happy men. So [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)much for these questions. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

11 [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

That the fortunes of descendants and of all a man's friends should [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)not affect his happiness at all seems a very unfriendly doctrine, and one [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)opposed to the opinions men hold; but since the events that happen are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)numerous and admit of all sorts of difference, and some come more near [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)to us and others less so, it seems a long- nay, an infinite- task to discuss [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)each in detail; a general outline will perhaps suffice. If, then, as some [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)of a man's own misadventures have a certain weight and influence on life [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)while others are, as it were, lighter, so too there are differences among [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the misadventures of our friends taken as a whole, and it makes a difference [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)whether the various suffering befall the living or the dead (much more [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)even than whether lawless and terrible deeds are presupposed in a tragedy [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)or done on the stage), this difference also must be taken into account; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)or rather, perhaps, the fact that doubt is felt whether the dead share [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)in any good or evil. For it seems, from these considerations, that even [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)if anything whether good or evil penetrates to them, it must be something [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)weak and negligible, either in itself or for them, or if not, at least [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)it must be such in degree and kind as not to make happy those who are not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)happy nor to take away their blessedness from those who are. The good or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)bad fortunes of friends, then, seem to have some effects on the dead, but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)effects of such a kind and degree as neither to make the happy unhappy [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)nor to produce any other change of the kind. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

12 [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)

These questions having been definitely answered, let us consider [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)whether happiness is among the things that are praised or rather among [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the things that are prized; for clearly it is not to be placed among potentialities. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)Everything that is praised seems to be praised because it is of a certain [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)kind and is related somehow to something else; for we praise the just or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)brave man and in general both the good man and virtue itself because of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)the actions and functions involved, and we praise the strong man, the good [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)runner, and so on, because he is of a certain kind and is related in a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.1.i.html)certain way to something good and important.
