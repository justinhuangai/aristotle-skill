**Politics**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

By Aristotle [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Written 350 B.C.E

Translated by Benjamin Jowett

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)**Part I**

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)Every state is a community of some kind, and every community is established [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)with a view to some good; for mankind always act in order to obtain that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)which they think good. But, if all communities aim at some good, the state [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)or political community, which is the highest of all, and which embraces [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)all the rest, aims at good in a greater degree than any other, and at the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)highest good. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Some people think that the qualifications of a statesman, king, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)householder, and master are the same, and that they differ, not in kind, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)but only in the number of their subjects. For example, the ruler over a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)few is called a master; over more, the manager of a household; over a still [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)larger number, a statesman or king, as if there were no difference between [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)a great household and a small state. The distinction which is made between [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the king and the statesman is as follows: When the government is personal, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the ruler is a king; when, according to the rules of the political science, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the citizens rule and are ruled in turn, then he is called a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)statesman. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

But all this is a mistake; for governments differ in kind, as will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)be evident to any one who considers the matter according to the method [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)which has hitherto guided us. As in other departments of science, so in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)politics, the compound should always be resolved into the simple elements [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)or least parts of the whole. We must therefore look at the elements of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)which the state is composed, in order that we may see in what the different [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)kinds of rule differ from one another, and whether any scientific result [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)can be attained about each one of them.

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)**Part II**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

He who thus considers things in their first growth and origin, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)whether a state or anything else, will obtain the clearest view of them. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)In the first place there must be a union of those who cannot exist without [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)each other; namely, of male and female, that the race may continue (and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)this is a union which is formed, not of deliberate purpose, but because, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)in common with other animals and with plants, mankind have a natural desire [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)to leave behind them an image of themselves), and of natural ruler and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)subject, that both may be preserved. For that which can foresee by the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)exercise of mind is by nature intended to be lord and master, and that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)which can with its body give effect to such foresight is a subject, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)by nature a slave; hence master and slave have the same interest. Now nature [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)has distinguished between the female and the slave. For she is not niggardly, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)like the smith who fashions the Delphian knife for many uses; she makes [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)each thing for a single use, and every instrument is best made when intended [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)for one and not for many uses. But among barbarians no distinction is made [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)between women and slaves, because there is no natural ruler among them: [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)they are a community of slaves, male and female. Wherefore the poets say, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

"It is meet that Hellenes should rule over barbarians; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)"

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)as if they thought that the barbarian and the slave were by nature [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)one. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Out of these two relationships between man and woman, master and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)slave, the first thing to arise is the family, and Hesiod is right when [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)he says,

"First house and wife and an ox for the plough, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)"

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)for the ox is the poor man's slave. The family is the association established [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)by nature for the supply of men's everyday wants, and the members of it [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)are called by Charondas 'companions of the cupboard,' and by Epimenides [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the Cretan, 'companions of the manger.' But when several families are united, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and the association aims at something more than the supply of daily needs, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the first society to be formed is the village. And the most natural form [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of the village appears to be that of a colony from the family, composed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of the children and grandchildren, who are said to be suckled 'with the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)same milk.' And this is the reason why Hellenic states were originally [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)governed by kings; because the Hellenes were under royal rule before they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)came together, as the barbarians still are. Every family is ruled by the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)eldest, and therefore in the colonies of the family the kingly form of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)government prevailed because they were of the same blood. As Homer says: [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

"Each one gives law to his children and to his wives. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)"

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)For they lived dispersedly, as was the manner in ancient times. Wherefore [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)men say that the Gods have a king, because they themselves either are or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)were in ancient times under the rule of a king. For they imagine, not only [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the forms of the Gods, but their ways of life to be like their [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)own. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

When several villages are united in a single complete community, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)large enough to be nearly or quite self-sufficing, the state comes into [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)existence, originating in the bare needs of life, and continuing in existence [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)for the sake of a good life. And therefore, if the earlier forms of society [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)are natural, so is the state, for it is the end of them, and the nature [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of a thing is its end. For what each thing is when fully developed, we [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)call its nature, whether we are speaking of a man, a horse, or a family. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)Besides, the final cause and end of a thing is the best, and to be self-sufficing [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is the end and the best. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Hence it is evident that the state is a creation of nature, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)that man is by nature a political animal. And he who by nature and not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)by mere accident is without a state, is either a bad man or above humanity; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)he is like the

"Tribeless, lawless, hearthless one, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)"

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)whom Homer denounces- the natural outcast is forthwith a lover of war; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)he may be compared to an isolated piece at draughts. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Now, that man is more of a political animal than bees or any other [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)gregarious animals is evident. Nature, as we often say, makes nothing in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)vain, and man is the only animal whom she has endowed with the gift of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)speech. And whereas mere voice is but an indication of pleasure or pain, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and is therefore found in other animals (for their nature attains to the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)perception of pleasure and pain and the intimation of them to one another, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and no further), the power of speech is intended to set forth the expedient [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and inexpedient, and therefore likewise the just and the unjust. And it [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is a characteristic of man that he alone has any sense of good and evil, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of just and unjust, and the like, and the association of living beings [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)who have this sense makes a family and a state. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Further, the state is by nature clearly prior to the family and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)to the individual, since the whole is of necessity prior to the part; for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)example, if the whole body be destroyed, there will be no foot or hand, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)except in an equivocal sense, as we might speak of a stone hand; for when [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)destroyed the hand will be no better than that. But things are defined [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)by their working and power; and we ought not to say that they are the same [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)when they no longer have their proper quality, but only that they have [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the same name. The proof that the state is a creation of nature and prior [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)to the individual is that the individual, when isolated, is not self-sufficing; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and therefore he is like a part in relation to the whole. But he who is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)unable to live in society, or who has no need because he is sufficient [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)for himself, must be either a beast or a god: he is no part of a state. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)A social instinct is implanted in all men by nature, and yet he who first [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)founded the state was the greatest of benefactors. For man, when perfected, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is the best of animals, but, when separated from law and justice, he is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the worst of all; since armed injustice is the more dangerous, and he is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)equipped at birth with arms, meant to be used by intelligence and virtue, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)which he may use for the worst ends. Wherefore, if he have not virtue, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)he is the most unholy and the most savage of animals, and the most full [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of lust and gluttony. But justice is the bond of men in states, for the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)administration of justice, which is the determination of what is just, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is the principle of order in political society.

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)**Part III**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Seeing then that the state is made up of households, before speaking [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of the state we must speak of the management of the household. The parts [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of household management correspond to the persons who compose the household, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and a complete household consists of slaves and freemen. Now we should [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)begin by examining everything in its fewest possible elements; and the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)first and fewest possible parts of a family are master and slave, husband [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and wife, father and children. We have therefore to consider what each [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of these three relations is and ought to be: I mean the relation of master [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and servant, the marriage relation (the conjunction of man and wife has [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)no name of its own), and thirdly, the procreative relation (this also has [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)no proper name). And there is another element of a household, the so-called [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)art of getting wealth, which, according to some, is identical with household [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)management, according to others, a principal part of it; the nature of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)this art will also have to be considered by us. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Let us first speak of master and slave, looking to the needs of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)practical life and also seeking to attain some better theory of their relation [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)than exists at present. For some are of opinion that the rule of a master [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is a science, and that the management of a household, and the mastership [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of slaves, and the political and royal rule, as I was saying at the outset, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)are all the same. Others affirm that the rule of a master over slaves is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)contrary to nature, and that the distinction between slave and freeman [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)exists by law only, and not by nature; and being an interference with nature [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is therefore unjust.

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)**Part IV**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Property is a part of the household, and the art of acquiring property [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is a part of the art of managing the household; for no man can live well, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)or indeed live at all, unless he be provided with necessaries. And as in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the arts which have a definite sphere the workers must have their own proper [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)instruments for the accomplishment of their work, so it is in the management [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of a household. Now instruments are of various sorts; some are living, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)others lifeless; in the rudder, the pilot of a ship has a lifeless, in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the look-out man, a living instrument; for in the arts the servant is a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)kind of instrument. Thus, too, a possession is an instrument for maintaining [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)life. And so, in the arrangement of the family, a slave is a living possession, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and property a number of such instruments; and the servant is himself an [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)instrument which takes precedence of all other instruments. For if every [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)instrument could accomplish its own work, obeying or anticipating the will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of others, like the statues of Daedalus, or the tripods of Hephaestus, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)which, says the poet,

"of their own accord entered the assembly [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of the Gods; "

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)if, in like manner, the shuttle would weave and the plectrum touch the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)lyre without a hand to guide them, chief workmen would not want servants, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)nor masters slaves. Here, however, another distinction must be drawn; the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)instruments commonly so called are instruments of production, whilst a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)possession is an instrument of action. The shuttle, for example, is not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)only of use; but something else is made by it, whereas of a garment or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of a bed there is only the use. Further, as production and action are different [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)in kind, and both require instruments, the instruments which they employ [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)must likewise differ in kind. But life is action and not production, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)therefore the slave is the minister of action. Again, a possession is spoken [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of as a part is spoken of; for the part is not only a part of something [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)else, but wholly belongs to it; and this is also true of a possession. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)The master is only the master of the slave; he does not belong to him, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)whereas the slave is not only the slave of his master, but wholly belongs [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)to him. Hence we see what is the nature and office of a slave; he who is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)by nature not his own but another's man, is by nature a slave; and he may [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)be said to be another's man who, being a human being, is also a possession. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)And a possession may be defined as an instrument of action, separable from [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the possessor.

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)**Part V**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

But is there any one thus intended by nature to be a slave, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)for whom such a condition is expedient and right, or rather is not all [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)slavery a violation of nature? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

There is no difficulty in answering this question, on grounds both [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of reason and of fact. For that some should rule and others be ruled is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)a thing not only necessary, but expedient; from the hour of their birth, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)some are marked out for subjection, others for rule. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

And there are many kinds both of rulers and subjects (and that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)rule is the better which is exercised over better subjects- for example, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)to rule over men is better than to rule over wild beasts; for the work [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is better which is executed by better workmen, and where one man rules [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and another is ruled, they may be said to have a work); for in all things [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)which form a composite whole and which are made up of parts, whether continuous [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)or discrete, a distinction between the ruling and the subject element comes [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)to fight. Such a duality exists in living creatures, but not in them only; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)it originates in the constitution of the universe; even in things which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)have no life there is a ruling principle, as in a musical mode. But we [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)are wandering from the subject. We will therefore restrict ourselves to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the living creature, which, in the first place, consists of soul and body: [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and of these two, the one is by nature the ruler, and the other the subject. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)But then we must look for the intentions of nature in things which retain [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)their nature, and not in things which are corrupted. And therefore we must [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)study the man who is in the most perfect state both of body and soul, for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)in him we shall see the true relation of the two; although in bad or corrupted [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)natures the body will often appear to rule over the soul, because they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)are in an evil and unnatural condition. At all events we may firstly observe [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)in living creatures both a despotical and a constitutional rule; for the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)soul rules the body with a despotical rule, whereas the intellect rules [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the appetites with a constitutional and royal rule. And it is clear that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the rule of the soul over the body, and of the mind and the rational element [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)over the passionate, is natural and expedient; whereas the equality of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the two or the rule of the inferior is always hurtful. The same holds good [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of animals in relation to men; for tame animals have a better nature than [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)wild, and all tame animals are better off when they are ruled by man; for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)then they are preserved. Again, the male is by nature superior, and the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)female inferior; and the one rules, and the other is ruled; this principle, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of necessity, extends to all mankind. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Where then there is such a difference as that between soul and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)body, or between men and animals (as in the case of those whose business [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is to use their body, and who can do nothing better), the lower sort are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)by nature slaves, and it is better for them as for all inferiors that they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)should be under the rule of a master. For he who can be, and therefore [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is, another's and he who participates in rational principle enough to apprehend, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)but not to have, such a principle, is a slave by nature. Whereas the lower [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)animals cannot even apprehend a principle; they obey their instincts. And [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)indeed the use made of slaves and of tame animals is not very different; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)for both with their bodies minister to the needs of life. Nature would [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)like to distinguish between the bodies of freemen and slaves, making the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)one strong for servile labor, the other upright, and although useless for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)such services, useful for political life in the arts both of war and peace. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)But the opposite often happens- that some have the souls and others have [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the bodies of freemen. And doubtless if men differed from one another in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the mere forms of their bodies as much as the statues of the Gods do from [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)men, all would acknowledge that the inferior class should be slaves of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the superior. And if this is true of the body, how much more just that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)a similar distinction should exist in the soul? but the beauty of the body [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is seen, whereas the beauty of the soul is not seen. It is clear, then, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)that some men are by nature free, and others slaves, and that for these [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)latter slavery is both expedient and right.

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)**Part VI**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

But that those who take the opposite view have in a certain way [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)right on their side, may be easily seen. For the words slavery and slave [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)are used in two senses. There is a slave or slavery by law as well as by [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)nature. The law of which I speak is a sort of convention- the law by which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)whatever is taken in war is supposed to belong to the victors. But this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)right many jurists impeach, as they would an orator who brought forward [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)an unconstitutional measure: they detest the notion that, because one man [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)has the power of doing violence and is superior in brute strength, another [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)shall be his slave and subject. Even among philosophers there is a difference [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of opinion. The origin of the dispute, and what makes the views invade [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)each other's territory, is as follows: in some sense virtue, when furnished [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)with means, has actually the greatest power of exercising force; and as [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)superior power is only found where there is superior excellence of some [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)kind, power seems to imply virtue, and the dispute to be simply one about [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)justice (for it is due to one party identifying justice with goodwill while [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the other identifies it with the mere rule of the stronger). If these views [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)are thus set out separately, the other views have no force or plausibility [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)against the view that the superior in virtue ought to rule, or be master. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)Others, clinging, as they think, simply to a principle of justice (for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)law and custom are a sort of justice), assume that slavery in accordance [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)with the custom of war is justified by law, but at the same moment they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)deny this. For what if the cause of the war be unjust? And again, no one [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)would ever say he is a slave who is unworthy to be a slave. Were this the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)case, men of the highest rank would be slaves and the children of slaves [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)if they or their parents chance to have been taken captive and sold. Wherefore [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)Hellenes do not like to call Hellenes slaves, but confine the term to barbarians. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)Yet, in using this language, they really mean the natural slave of whom [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)we spoke at first; for it must be admitted that some are slaves everywhere, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)others nowhere. The same principle applies to nobility. Hellenes regard [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)themselves as noble everywhere, and not only in their own country, but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)they deem the barbarians noble only when at home, thereby implying that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)there are two sorts of nobility and freedom, the one absolute, the other [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)relative. The Helen of Theodectes says:

"Who would presume to call [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)me servant who am on both sides sprung from the stem of the Gods? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)"

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)What does this mean but that they distinguish freedom and slavery, noble [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and humble birth, by the two principles of good and evil? They think that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)as men and animals beget men and animals, so from good men a good man springs. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)But this is what nature, though she may intend it, cannot always [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)accomplish. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

We see then that there is some foundation for this difference of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)opinion, and that all are not either slaves by nature or freemen by nature, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and also that there is in some cases a marked distinction between the two [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)classes, rendering it expedient and right for the one to be slaves and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the others to be masters: the one practicing obedience, the others exercising [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the authority and lordship which nature intended them to have. The abuse [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of this authority is injurious to both; for the interests of part and whole, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of body and soul, are the same, and the slave is a part of the master, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)a living but separated part of his bodily frame. Hence, where the relation [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of master and slave between them is natural they are friends and have a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)common interest, but where it rests merely on law and force the reverse [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is true.

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)**Part VII**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

The previous remarks are quite enough to show that the rule of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)a master is not a constitutional rule, and that all the different kinds [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of rule are not, as some affirm, the same with each other. For there is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)one rule exercised over subjects who are by nature free, another over subjects [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)who are by nature slaves. The rule of a household is a monarchy, for every [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)house is under one head: whereas constitutional rule is a government of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)freemen and equals. The master is not called a master because he has science, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)but because he is of a certain character, and the same remark applies to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the slave and the freeman. Still there may be a science for the master [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and science for the slave. The science of the slave would be such as the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)man of Syracuse taught, who made money by instructing slaves in their ordinary [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)duties. And such a knowledge may be carried further, so as to include cookery [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and similar menial arts. For some duties are of the more necessary, others [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of the more honorable sort; as the proverb says, 'slave before slave, master [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)before master.' But all such branches of knowledge are servile. There is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)likewise a science of the master, which teaches the use of slaves; for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the master as such is concerned, not with the acquisition, but with the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)use of them. Yet this so-called science is not anything great or wonderful; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)for the master need only know how to order that which the slave must know [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)how to execute. Hence those who are in a position which places them above [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)toil have stewards who attend to their households while they occupy themselves [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)with philosophy or with politics. But the art of acquiring slaves, I mean [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of justly acquiring them, differs both from the art of the master and the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)art of the slave, being a species of hunting or war. Enough of the distinction [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)between master and slave.

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)**Part VIII**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Let us now inquire into property generally, and into the art of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)getting wealth, in accordance with our usual method, for a slave has been [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)shown to be a part of property. The first question is whether the art of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)getting wealth is the same with the art of managing a household or a part [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of it, or instrumental to it; and if the last, whether in the way that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the art of making shuttles is instrumental to the art of weaving, or in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the way that the casting of bronze is instrumental to the art of the statuary, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)for they are not instrumental in the same way, but the one provides tools [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and the other material; and by material I mean the substratum out of which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)any work is made; thus wool is the material of the weaver, bronze of the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)statuary. Now it is easy to see that the art of household management is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)not identical with the art of getting wealth, for the one uses the material [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)which the other provides. For the art which uses household stores can be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)no other than the art of household management. There is, however, a doubt [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)whether the art of getting wealth is a part of household management or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)a distinct art. If the getter of wealth has to consider whence wealth and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)property can be procured, but there are many sorts of property and riches, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)then are husbandry, and the care and provision of food in general, parts [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of the wealth-getting art or distinct arts? Again, there are many sorts [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of food, and therefore there are many kinds of lives both of animals and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)men; they must all have food, and the differences in their food have made [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)differences in their ways of life. For of beasts, some are gregarious, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)others are solitary; they live in the way which is best adapted to sustain [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)them, accordingly as they are carnivorous or herbivorous or omnivorous: [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and their habits are determined for them by nature in such a manner that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)they may obtain with greater facility the food of their choice. But, as [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)different species have different tastes, the same things are not naturally [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)pleasant to all of them; and therefore the lives of carnivorous or herbivorous [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)animals further differ among themselves. In the lives of men too there [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is a great difference. The laziest are shepherds, who lead an idle life, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and get their subsistence without trouble from tame animals; their flocks [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)having to wander from place to place in search of pasture, they are compelled [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)to follow them, cultivating a sort of living farm. Others support themselves [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)by hunting, which is of different kinds. Some, for example, are brigands, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)others, who dwell near lakes or marshes or rivers or a sea in which there [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)are fish, are fishermen, and others live by the pursuit of birds or wild [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)beasts. The greater number obtain a living from the cultivated fruits of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the soil. Such are the modes of subsistence which prevail among those whose [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)industry springs up of itself, and whose food is not acquired by exchange [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and retail trade- there is the shepherd, the husbandman, the brigand, the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)fisherman, the hunter. Some gain a comfortable maintenance out of two employments, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)eking out the deficiencies of one of them by another: thus the life of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)a shepherd may be combined with that of a brigand, the life of a farmer [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)with that of a hunter. Other modes of life are similarly combined in any [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)way which the needs of men may require. Property, in the sense of a bare [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)livelihood, seems to be given by nature herself to all, both when they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)are first born, and when they are grown up. For some animals bring forth, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)together with their offspring, so much food as will last until they are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)able to supply themselves; of this the vermiparous or oviparous animals [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)are an instance; and the viviparous animals have up to a certain time a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)supply of food for their young in themselves, which is called milk. In [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)like manner we may infer that, after the birth of animals, plants exist [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)for their sake, and that the other animals exist for the sake of man, the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)tame for use and food, the wild, if not all at least the greater part of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)them, for food, and for the provision of clothing and various instruments. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)Now if nature makes nothing incomplete, and nothing in vain, the inference [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)must be that she has made all animals for the sake of man. And so, in one [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)point of view, the art of war is a natural art of acquisition, for the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)art of acquisition includes hunting, an art which we ought to practice [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)against wild beasts, and against men who, though intended by nature to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)be governed, will not submit; for war of such a kind is naturally [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)just. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Of the art of acquisition then there is one kind which by nature [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is a part of the management of a household, in so far as the art of household [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)management must either find ready to hand, or itself provide, such things [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)necessary to life, and useful for the community of the family or state, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)as can be stored. They are the elements of true riches; for the amount [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of property which is needed for a good life is not unlimited, although [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)Solon in one of his poems says that

"No bound to riches has been [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)fixed for man. "

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)But there is a boundary fixed, just as there is in the other arts; for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the instruments of any art are never unlimited, either in number or size, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and riches may be defined as a number of instruments to be used in a household [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)or in a state. And so we see that there is a natural art of acquisition [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)which is practiced by managers of households and by statesmen, and what [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is the reason of this.

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)**Part IX**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

There is another variety of the art of acquisition which is commonly [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and rightly called an art of wealth-getting, and has in fact suggested [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the notion that riches and property have no limit. Being nearly connected [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)with the preceding, it is often identified with it. But though they are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)not very different, neither are they the same. The kind already described [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is given by nature, the other is gained by experience and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)art. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Let us begin our discussion of the question with the following [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)considerations: [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Of everything which we possess there are two uses: both belong [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)to the thing as such, but not in the same manner, for one is the proper, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and the other the improper or secondary use of it. For example, a shoe [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is used for wear, and is used for exchange; both are uses of the shoe. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)He who gives a shoe in exchange for money or food to him who wants one, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)does indeed use the shoe as a shoe, but this is not its proper or primary [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)purpose, for a shoe is not made to be an object of barter. The same may [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)be said of all possessions, for the art of exchange extends to all of them, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and it arises at first from what is natural, from the circumstance that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)some have too little, others too much. Hence we may infer that retail trade [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is not a natural part of the art of getting wealth; had it been so, men [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)would have ceased to exchange when they had enough. In the first community, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)indeed, which is the family, this art is obviously of no use, but it begins [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)to be useful when the society increases. For the members of the family [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)originally had all things in common; later, when the family divided into [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)parts, the parts shared in many things, and different parts in different [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)things, which they had to give in exchange for what they wanted, a kind [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of barter which is still practiced among barbarous nations who exchange [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)with one another the necessaries of life and nothing more; giving and receiving [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)wine, for example, in exchange for coin, and the like. This sort of barter [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is not part of the wealth-getting art and is not contrary to nature, but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is needed for the satisfaction of men's natural wants. The other or more [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)complex form of exchange grew, as might have been inferred, out of the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)simpler. When the inhabitants of one country became more dependent on those [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of another, and they imported what they needed, and exported what they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)had too much of, money necessarily came into use. For the various necessaries [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of life are not easily carried about, and hence men agreed to employ in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)their dealings with each other something which was intrinsically useful [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and easily applicable to the purposes of life, for example, iron, silver, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and the like. Of this the value was at first measured simply by size and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)weight, but in process of time they put a stamp upon it, to save the trouble [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of weighing and to mark the value. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

When the use of coin had once been discovered, out of the barter [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of necessary articles arose the other art of wealth getting, namely, retail [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)trade; which was at first probably a simple matter, but became more complicated [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)as soon as men learned by experience whence and by what exchanges the greatest [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)profit might be made. Originating in the use of coin, the art of getting [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)wealth is generally thought to be chiefly concerned with it, and to be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the art which produces riches and wealth; having to consider how they may [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)be accumulated. Indeed, riches is assumed by many to be only a quantity [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of coin, because the arts of getting wealth and retail trade are concerned [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)with coin. Others maintain that coined money is a mere sham, a thing not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)natural, but conventional only, because, if the users substitute another [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)commodity for it, it is worthless, and because it is not useful as a means [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)to any of the necessities of life, and, indeed, he who is rich in coin [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)may often be in want of necessary food. But how can that be wealth of which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)a man may have a great abundance and yet perish with hunger, like Midas [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)in the fable, whose insatiable prayer turned everything that was set before [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)him into gold? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Hence men seek after a better notion of riches and of the art of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)getting wealth than the mere acquisition of coin, and they are right. For [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)natural riches and the natural art of wealth-getting are a different thing; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)in their true form they are part of the management of a household; whereas [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)retail trade is the art of producing wealth, not in every way, but by exchange. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)And it is thought to be concerned with coin; for coin is the unit of exchange [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and the measure or limit of it. And there is no bound to the riches which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)spring from this art of wealth getting. As in the art of medicine there [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is no limit to the pursuit of health, and as in the other arts there is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)no limit to the pursuit of their several ends, for they aim at accomplishing [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)their ends to the uttermost (but of the means there is a limit, for the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)end is always the limit), so, too, in this art of wealth-getting there [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is no limit of the end, which is riches of the spurious kind, and the acquisition [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of wealth. But the art of wealth-getting which consists in household management, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)on the other hand, has a limit; the unlimited acquisition of wealth is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)not its business. And, therefore, in one point of view, all riches must [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)have a limit; nevertheless, as a matter of fact, we find the opposite to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)be the case; for all getters of wealth increase their hoard of coin without [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)limit. The source of the confusion is the near connection between the two [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)kinds of wealth-getting; in either, the instrument is the same, although [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the use is different, and so they pass into one another; for each is a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)use of the same property, but with a difference: accumulation is the end [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)in the one case, but there is a further end in the other. Hence some persons [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)are led to believe that getting wealth is the object of household management, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and the whole idea of their lives is that they ought either to increase [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)their money without limit, or at any rate not to lose it. The origin of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)this disposition in men is that they are intent upon living only, and not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)upon living well; and, as their desires are unlimited they also desire [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)that the means of gratifying them should be without limit. Those who do [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)aim at a good life seek the means of obtaining bodily pleasures; and, since [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the enjoyment of these appears to depend on property, they are absorbed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)in getting wealth: and so there arises the second species of wealth-getting. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)For, as their enjoyment is in excess, they seek an art which produces the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)excess of enjoyment; and, if they are not able to supply their pleasures [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)by the art of getting wealth, they try other arts, using in turn every [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)faculty in a manner contrary to nature. The quality of courage, for example, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is not intended to make wealth, but to inspire confidence; neither is this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the aim of the general's or of the physician's art; but the one aims at [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)victory and the other at health. Nevertheless, some men turn every quality [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)or art into a means of getting wealth; this they conceive to be the end, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and to the promotion of the end they think all things must [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)contribute. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Thus, then, we have considered the art of wealth-getting which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is unnecessary, and why men want it; and also the necessary art of wealth-getting, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)which we have seen to be different from the other, and to be a natural [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)part of the art of managing a household, concerned with the provision of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)food, not, however, like the former kind, unlimited, but having a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)limit.

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)**Part X**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

And we have found the answer to our original question, Whether [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the art of getting wealth is the business of the manager of a household [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and of the statesman or not their business? viz., that wealth is presupposed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)by them. For as political science does not make men, but takes them from [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)nature and uses them, so too nature provides them with earth or sea or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the like as a source of food. At this stage begins the duty of the manager [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of a household, who has to order the things which nature supplies; he may [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)be compared to the weaver who has not to make but to use wool, and to know, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)too, what sort of wool is good and serviceable or bad and unserviceable. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)Were this otherwise, it would be difficult to see why the art of getting [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)wealth is a part of the management of a household and the art of medicine [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)not; for surely the members of a household must have health just as they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)must have life or any other necessary. The answer is that as from one point [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of view the master of the house and the ruler of the state have to consider [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)about health, from another point of view not they but the physician; so [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)in one way the art of household management, in another way the subordinate [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)art, has to consider about wealth. But, strictly speaking, as I have already [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)said, the means of life must be provided beforehand by nature; for the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)business of nature is to furnish food to that which is born, and the food [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of the offspring is always what remains over of that from which it is produced. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)Wherefore the art of getting wealth out of fruits and animals is always [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)natural. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

There are two sorts of wealth-getting, as I have said; one is a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)part of household management, the other is retail trade: the former necessary [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and honorable, while that which consists in exchange is justly censured; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)for it is unnatural, and a mode by which men gain from one another. The [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)most hated sort, and with the greatest reason, is usury, which makes a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)gain out of money itself, and not from the natural object of it. For money [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)was intended to be used in exchange, but not to increase at interest. And [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)this term interest, which means the birth of money from money, is applied [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)to the breeding of money because the offspring resembles the parent. Wherefore [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of an modes of getting wealth this is the most unnatural.

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)**Part XI**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Enough has been said about the theory of wealth-getting; we will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)now proceed to the practical part. The discussion of such matters is not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)unworthy of philosophy, but to be engaged in them practically is illiberal [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and irksome. The useful parts of wealth-getting are, first, the knowledge [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of livestock- which are most profitable, and where, and how- as, for example, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)what sort of horses or sheep or oxen or any other animals are most likely [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)to give a return. A man ought to know which of these pay better than others, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and which pay best in particular places, for some do better in one place [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and some in another. Secondly, husbandry, which may be either tillage or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)planting, and the keeping of bees and of fish, or fowl, or of any animals [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)which may be useful to man. These are the divisions of the true or proper [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)art of wealth-getting and come first. Of the other, which consists in exchange, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the first and most important division is commerce (of which there are three [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)kinds- the provision of a ship, the conveyance of goods, exposure for sale- [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)these again differing as they are safer or more profitable), the second [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is usury, the third, service for hire- of this, one kind is employed in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the mechanical arts, the other in unskilled and bodily labor. There is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)still a third sort of wealth getting intermediate between this and the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)first or natural mode which is partly natural, but is also concerned with [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)exchange, viz., the industries that make their profit from the earth, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)from things growing from the earth which, although they bear no fruit, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)are nevertheless profitable; for example, the cutting of timber and all [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)mining. The art of mining, by which minerals are obtained, itself has many [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)branches, for there are various kinds of things dug out of the earth. Of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the several divisions of wealth-getting I now speak generally; a minute [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)consideration of them might be useful in practice, but it would be tiresome [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)to dwell upon them at greater length now. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Those occupations are most truly arts in which there is the least [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)element of chance; they are the meanest in which the body is most deteriorated, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)the most servile in which there is the greatest use of the body, and the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)most illiberal in which there is the least need of excellence. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)

Works have been written upon these subjects by various persons; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)for example, by Chares the Parian, and Apollodorus the Lemnian, who have [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)treated of Tillage and Planting, while others have treated of other branches; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)any one who cares for such matters may refer to their writings. It would [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)be well also to collect the scattered stories of the ways in which individuals [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)have succeeded in amassing a fortune; for all this is useful to persons [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)who value the art of getting wealth. There is the anecdote of Thales the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)Milesian and his financial device, which involves a principle of universal [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)application, but is attributed to him on account of his reputation for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)wisdom. He was reproached for his poverty, which was supposed to show that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)philosophy was of no use. According to the story, he knew by his skill [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)in the stars while it was yet winter that there would be a great harvest [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of olives in the coming year; so, having a little money, he gave deposits [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)for the use of all the olive-presses in Chios and Miletus, which he hired [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)at a low price because no one bid against him. When the harvest-time came, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)and many were wanted all at once and of a sudden, he let them out at any [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)rate which he pleased, and made a quantity of money. Thus he showed the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)world that philosophers can easily be rich if they like, but that their [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)ambition is of another sort. He is supposed to have given a striking proof [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)of his wisdom, but, as I was saying, his device for getting wealth is of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)universal application, and is nothing but the creation of a monopoly. It [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)is an art often practiced by cities when they are want of money; they make [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)a monopoly of provisions. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.1.one.html)
