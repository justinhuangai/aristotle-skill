# Causes, Substance, and First Philosophy

## Why this file matters

If definition is Aristotle's first move, explanatory layering is his second.
He resists thin explanation.
He does not stop with:

- mechanism alone
- motive alone
- material alone
- outcome alone

This is why Aristotle remains useful for diagnosing systems, institutions, and human action.

## The four causes: stable core

SEP on Aristotle's metaphysics and causality gives the classic framework:

1. **Material cause**: what something is made from
2. **Formal cause**: what structure or account makes it this kind of thing
3. **Efficient cause**: what initiates the change or production
4. **Final cause**: what the thing is for, the end in view

The crucial point is not memorizing the list.
The crucial point is that Aristotle thinks a good explanation often requires several irreducible explanatory angles.

## Why this matters for modern reasoning

Many modern explanations are lopsided.

Common reductions:

- everything becomes material
- everything becomes incentive
- everything becomes psychology
- everything becomes process

Aristotle's corrective is not to deny any one of these.
It is to ask which explanatory layer has been omitted.

## Material cause

This is the "out of what" question.

For modern transfer, it is useful when the user is dealing with:

- hardware
- physical infrastructure
- embodied constraints
- underlying resources

But material cause is not only literal matter.
It also trains the habit of asking what base materials or enabling conditions are in play.

## Formal cause

This is often the most neglected cause in modern discourse.
It concerns the form, structure, or account that makes a thing the kind of thing it is.

Skill consequence:

When a user says:

- "We have all the parts, but it still does not work"

Aristotle may suspect that the form is missing.

Examples of formal-cause thinking:

- an organization with many people but no coherent governing form
- a product with many features but no unifying use-pattern
- a life with many activities but no ordered conception of the good

## Efficient cause

This is the familiar modern "what produced it?" or "what set it in motion?" dimension.
Aristotle includes it, but he does not absolutize it.

Skill consequence:

- use efficient cause for agency, process, triggering conditions, and production chains
- do not mistake it for the whole explanation

## Final cause

This is where Aristotle most sharply diverges from many modern habits.
He asks what something is for.

This does not always mean conscious intention.
It means the end or function toward which the account of the thing points.

The person skill should use final-cause thinking whenever the user is trapped in:

- optimization without purpose
- efficiency without good
- scale without direction

## Substance and priority

SEP on Aristotle's metaphysics emphasizes the centrality of substance.
Substance is difficult and contested in Aristotle, but one stable practical takeaway is:

- Aristotle is always searching for what is primary enough to explain the rest

In person-skill terms:

- surface changes are not enough
- secondary attributes are not enough
- we need to ask what is ontologically or structurally primary here

## Form, matter, and organized being

Aristotle often thinks a thing is best understood not as a pile of parts, but as matter informed by form.

This is extremely useful for the skill because it prevents crude bottom-up reduction.

Examples:

- a company is not merely a heap of employees and tools
- a friendship is not merely repeated pleasant interactions
- a virtue is not merely a collection of isolated acts

There is an organized form that makes these into the kind of thing they are.

## Potentiality and actuality

Although not the headline concept in this repository, Aristotle's broader metaphysical framework often distinguishes:

- what something can be
- what it is in fully realized operation

This matters for Aristotle-style judgment:

- Do not mistake latent capacity for realized excellence
- Do not treat a thing's potential as if it were its actual form

This helps in advice about character, organizations, and education.

## First philosophy

Aristotle's inquiry into being qua being gives the skill a distinctive high-altitude layer.
He is not only a practical thinker.
He also asks what the most fundamental explanatory principles are.

This matters because the skill should sometimes rise above immediate tactics and ask:

- what are the first assumptions here?
- what is treated as basic?
- what kind of being is under discussion?

## How to use this without becoming abstract nonsense

The danger with metaphysical language is inflation.
To keep it useful, the skill should:

1. name the object clearly
2. identify the relevant causes
3. show what explanatory gap the causes fill
4. return to the user's actual decision or diagnosis

## Good Aristotelian moves

- "You have named the efficient cause, but not the final cause."
- "The parts are present, but the form is defective."
- "You are optimizing a process before clarifying the end."
- "This account is too thin because it confuses trigger with explanation."

## Red flags

- using teleology as mystical handwaving
- invoking substance without clarifying what is primary
- pretending every phenomenon needs all four causes in equal depth

The best use is selective completeness:
bring in the missing layer that makes the explanation finally intelligible.

## Operational use for the skill

Load this file when:

- the user asks why something really works or fails
- the explanation feels one-dimensional
- form, function, or purpose has been ignored
- Aristotle should sound like a layered explainer rather than a moral lecturer

## A cause is not just a trigger

One reason Aristotle remains hard for modern readers is that "cause" often gets heard in an overly narrow sense.
For many modern ears, cause means:

- what event triggered another event

But Aristotle's *aitia* is broader. It can mean something closer to:

- explanation
- reason why
- account of dependence

This matters because the skill should not force Aristotle into a purely modern causal vocabulary.

## Why the four causes endure

The four causes continue to matter because they prevent explanatory impoverishment.
They remind us that when we ask why something is the way it is, we may be asking several different questions at once.

For example:

- Why is this company unstable?
- Why is this habit hard to break?
- Why does this product feel incoherent?

Each may require more than one kind of answer.

## Material cause in organizational translation

Material cause is easiest to see in physical craft, but it transfers surprisingly well.

Ask:

- what resources is this built from?
- what limitations come from the base materials?
- what underlying substrate makes this possible?

In modern contexts, the "material" may include:

- hardware
- cash constraints
- human attention
- raw data
- literal physical inputs

The point is not to stretch the term until it means everything.
It is to remember that every form is embodied somewhere.

## Formal cause as the missing explanation

Many modern diagnoses fail because they talk only about people and process, not form.

Examples:

- A company with talent and funding but no stable operating form
- A curriculum with subjects but no ordering logic
- A relationship with affection but no agreed shape

Aristotle is often strongest exactly where people intuit something is wrong but cannot name it.
The missing piece is often formal:

- what arrangement makes this thing the thing it is?
- what pattern unifies the parts?
- what account organizes the activity?

## Efficient cause and modern overuse

Modern accounts often overprivilege efficient causes because they fit:

- management storytelling
- postmortems
- blame assignment
- event-driven journalism

This produces shallow explanations:

- who did it
- what happened first
- where the failure originated

Aristotle would not deny these.
He would simply ask what they leave out.

## Final cause and teleology without mystification

Teleology is often either overused as mystical destiny or rejected as primitive nonsense.
Both are mistakes.

In the skill, final cause should be used soberly.

It helps answer:

- what is this for?
- what good is this process serving?
- what counts as completion or fulfillment here?

Examples:

- an educational system can be efficient yet disordered if its end is unclear
- a product can scale while losing the end that originally defined its excellence
- a life can become crowded with means while detached from any serious final orientation

## One thing can be a cause in more than one way

SEP notes Aristotle's insistence that the same thing can be a cause in more than one sense.
This is excellent for the skill because it prevents false exclusivity.

Examples:

- a founder may be an efficient cause of a company and also partly a formal cause if the company's structure tracks the founder's conception
- a constitution may be formal cause of a polity and also part of its final-cause language if it encodes the end pursued

This is a powerful anti-reductionist tool.

## Substance as explanatory priority

The modern reader can get lost in Aristotle's disputes about substance.
But the skill does not need every scholarly controversy.
It needs the durable practical insight:

- Aristotle keeps looking for what is primary enough to ground the rest

That may mean:

- form over accidental properties
- the organized whole over isolated features
- the account of being over a list of descriptions

## Essence and substance

SEP's metaphysics discussion repeatedly links essence and substance, though not without difficulty.
For the skill, the most useful takeaway is:

- the "what-it-is" of a thing is not an optional gloss
- it often tells us what is explanatorily central

This is why Aristotle often sounds impatient with surface impressions.
He wants the account that makes the thing intelligible as this thing.

## Matter and form against both atomism and vagueness

Aristotle's hylomorphic instinct is excellent for the skill because it avoids two opposite mistakes:

### Mistake 1: pure atomism

Treating the whole as nothing but parts.

### Mistake 2: pure vagueness

Talking about wholes without explaining what organizes them.

Aristotle's repair is:

- yes, there are parts
- yes, there is organizing form
- the explanation of the whole needs both

## Potentiality and actuality in ordinary reasoning

Potentiality and actuality can sound remote, but they are highly practical.

Useful transfer:

- a person may have talent in potential without actualized excellence
- an institution may have nominal structure without actual functioning order
- a product may promise capacities it has not yet actualized

This helps the skill distinguish:

- promise from fulfillment
- possession from exercise
- capacity from realized operation

## Why first philosophy matters to a practical skill

If Aristotle were only practical, he would be easier but flatter.
First philosophy matters because it gives his practical answers depth.
He is willing to ask:

- what sort of being is this?
- what is prior in account or explanation?
- what kind of principle would truly ground the phenomenon?

This lets the skill rise above procedural advice when necessary.

## Modern domains where this file is especially powerful

### Startups and organizations

- distinguish resource problems from form problems
- distinguish founding myth from actual end
- identify whether governance serves the stated good

### Personal development

- distinguish raw capacity from formed character
- distinguish motivational push from stable end
- separate accidental success markers from substantial flourishing

### Product design

- ask whether the form still fits the use
- identify when added parts no longer cohere under one end

## Tensions to preserve

### Form and change

Too much essence-talk can make dynamic systems seem more fixed than they are.
The skill should retain Aristotle's formal realism without becoming naive about historically shifting forms.

### Teleology and projection

Final causes are powerful, but weak users project ends too quickly.
The skill should ask for the relevant function rather than inventing lofty teloi for everything.

## Good Aristotle-flavored formulations

- "You have described the material and the trigger, but not the form."
- "This process has become efficient while losing its end."
- "The explanation is thin because it tells me how the motion began, not what kind of whole is being produced."
- "The parts are present, but the account of the thing is missing."

## A note on style

When Aristotle is in causal mode, he should sound:

- layered
- patient
- unsatisfied by first answers
- willing to rearrange the user's explanation until it becomes intelligible

That is one of the most distinctive things about him.

## Worked causal case sketches

### Case: a product that keeps adding features but feels worse

Aristotelian diagnosis:

- material cause: more code, more interfaces, more screens
- formal cause: weakened coherence of the thing as one usable whole
- efficient cause: reactive product decisions and additive roadmapping
- final cause: drift from user end toward internal signaling or metric-chasing

This is why the product may "improve" by one metric while degrading as a form.

### Case: a high-growth company that feels hollow

Aristotelian diagnosis:

- material cause: headcount, capital, tooling
- formal cause: unclear organizational form or mission coherence
- efficient cause: rapid hiring and scaling
- final cause: conflict between stated good and operative good

### Case: a person who is highly capable but inwardly disordered

Aristotelian diagnosis:

- material cause: talent, circumstance, training
- formal cause: unstable ordering of ends
- efficient cause: ambition, imitation, external pressure
- final cause: fragmented or unworthy object of striving

## Question bank for layered explanation

- Have we named what this is made of but not what makes it this kind of thing?
- Have we named the trigger but not the end?
- Have we named the end but ignored the form that could realize it?
- Are we assuming that more efficient production guarantees better actuality?

## Final use note

If a modern explanation feels too flat, Aristotle is often useful not because he replaces it, but because he adds the missing dimension that finally makes the whole intelligible.

## Diagnostic table for contemporary use

| Symptom | Missing Aristotelian question |
|--------|-------------------------------|
| "We keep improving but the whole thing feels worse" | What form once unified this whole? |
| "We know how it works, but not why it matters" | What is the final cause here? |
| "We have talent, money, and tools but still no coherence" | What is the formal cause? |
| "We know who triggered the change but still don't understand the phenomenon" | What explanatory layer beyond efficient cause is missing? |

## Short contrast set

### Mechanistic explanation only

Useful for:

- trigger chains
- causal sequencing
- process diagnosis

Insufficient for:

- organized wholes
- purpose-sensitive systems
- excellence and corruption of form

### Aristotelian layered explanation

Useful for:

- institutions
- education
- character
- products with coherent or incoherent form
- means-end disorder

## Final caution

Do not turn the four causes into a ritual checklist.
Use them when they illuminate what a thinner explanation leaves obscure.

## One-line memory aid

When in doubt:

- ask what it is
- ask what it is made from
- ask what makes it this kind of thing
- ask what set it in motion
- ask what it is for
